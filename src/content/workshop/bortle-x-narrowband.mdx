If you've ever looked at the Bortle scale and felt despair at your suburban sky, this one's for you. I shoot from a Bortle X backyard in Suffolk — that's about as light-polluted as it gets in the UK outside central London. And yet, with narrowband filters and enough integration time, the results can compete with much darker sites.

## Why Narrowband Works

Narrowband filters (Ha, OIII, SII) only pass light at very specific wavelengths — the emission lines of ionised gases in nebulae. This means they reject almost all light pollution, which is broadband. Your 7nm Ha filter doesn't care about the sodium streetlight next door. It only sees hydrogen-alpha at 656.28nm.

## The Gear

My current rig:
- **Telescope**: Sky-Watcher Esprit 100ED (f/5.5, 550mm focal length)
- **Camera**: ZWO ASI533MM Pro (mono, square sensor, no amp glow)
- **Mount**: Sky-Watcher EQ6-R Pro (belt-modded)
- **Filters**: Antlia 3nm narrowband (Ha, OIII, SII)
- **Guide**: ZWO ASI120MM Mini + ZWO 30mm guide scope

The ASI533MM Pro is a cracking camera for this. Square sensor means no rotation worries, mono means proper narrowband capture, and the cooling keeps thermal noise predictable across long sessions.

## Technique

The key principles for Bortle X narrowband:

1. **Integration time is king.** More data always wins. I aim for 10+ hours per channel minimum.
2. **Dithering is essential.** Dither between every frame to remove walking noise patterns.
3. **5-minute subs are the sweet spot.** Long enough to build signal, short enough that the occasional satellite or gust doesn't ruin too many frames.
4. **Calibration matters more than you think.** Proper darks, flats, and dark flats. No shortcuts.

## The Results

From this setup, I regularly pull out targets that would be invisible to broadband cameras from my location. The Tadpoles, Heart, Soul, Rosette — all shot from a backyard where you can barely see Polaris with the naked eye.

The point isn't to compete with Bortle 1 sites. It's to prove that you don't need dark skies to do meaningful astrophotography. You just need patience, good filters, and a willingness to stack until the sun comes up.
