There's a specific kind of misery that comes with imaging on a tripod.

You've spotted a break in the clouds. The forecast shows three hours of clear sky before the next band of murk rolls in. Your target is perfectly placed, right in the imaging window you've been waiting weeks for. So you head out into the garden, haul the mount and scope out of the shed, level the tripod, rough-align on Polaris, wait for NINA's Three Point Polar Alignment to spin up, tweak the altitude bolts, tweak the azimuth bolts, do it again because you nudged it getting a better angle, and by the time you've got a decent polar alignment sorted, you've burned through forty minutes of your precious three-hour window.

If you're younger than me, perhaps that's fine. If, like me, you're an old git who doesn't particularly enjoy fumbling around in the dark with a head torch and arthritic fingers, it gets old remarkably fast.

## The Problem With Tripods

The core issue is repeatability. Or rather, the complete lack of it. Every single session, you're starting from scratch. You can get very good at polar alignment. You can run it until you could do it in your sleep. But you're still doing it. Every time. Without exception.

The time adds up too. Half an hour per session might not sound devastating, but across a season of twenty clear nights, that's ten hours of your life spent pointing at Polaris before you've captured a single photon of anything worth keeping.

Our garden isn't large enough for a proper observatory. A roll-off roof structure is out of the question, and a dome would require planning permission, structural groundwork, and a level of commitment to a single location that I'm not ready for. But during our recent house extension project, I spotted an opportunity. There was heavy machinery on site. There was already an open trench. A concrete pour was happening regardless.

It would have been almost rude not to take advantage of it.

## Digging In

I had the contractors dig out a 60cm cube hole at the edge of the patio. Into that went concrete, a decent amount of it, tamped properly and left to cure. The plan was straightforward: once set, I'd anchor a second-hand Altair Astro steel pier into the slab using shield anchors, do one very careful polar alignment, and then leave it alone indefinitely.

Simple in theory. There's always a bit of faff in practice.

Getting the shield anchors into properly cured concrete with the pier positioned accurately is one of those jobs that looks easy on YouTube and is genuinely fiddly in real life. The key is getting the bolt pattern right before you start drilling, because once those anchors are set you're not easily moving them. I took my time, double-checked the measurements, and resisted the urge to rush it.

Once the pier was bolted down and the mount head attached, I spent a proper session doing the polar alignment. Not a quick rough-and-ready one. A proper, careful, iterative alignment using PHD2's drift alignment method alongside NINA's Three Point. Then I fitted the Telegizmo cover, walked away, and vowed not to touch the azimuth or altitude adjusters unless something catastrophic happened.

## The Result

It's been transformative. That's not an exaggeration.

The setup time for my smaller William Optics FLT91 rig is now around four minutes. Carry scope out, attach to mount, balance, power everything up, let NINA sync on a nearby star, and start imaging. Four minutes. The polar alignment is already done. It was done weeks ago. It'll still be done next month.

The larger Askar 130 PHQ takes a bit longer, mostly because it's a genuinely hefty piece of kit that requires careful balancing and additional counterweights. Even so, I'm imaging within ten minutes of deciding to go out. That's a completely different relationship with the hobby. Spontaneous sessions are now possible. When there's an unexpected window in the clouds at 11pm, I go out. When the seeing looks good for thirty minutes before work, I go out. Before the pier, I'd have weighed up whether the setup time was worth it and often decided it wasn't.

It also sits on the mount permanently. The scopes have their accessories attached, the cables are routed and tied, the dew heaters are connected. Nothing needs to be rebuilt from session to session. The only thing that changes is which scope I'm attaching.

## Worth Every Bit of Faff

The timing was bittersweet, as these things tend to be. I only got it finished and commissioned as we were sliding towards the end of the astronomy season. A few decent nights, and then the British summer arrived to ruin everything with its endless atmospheric murk and short nights.

But that's fine. It's waiting. The polar alignment is still good. The concrete isn't going anywhere. When autumn arrives and the nights stretch back out, I'll be under dark skies within four minutes of deciding I want to be.

If you've got any possibility of putting in a permanent pier, even a modest one, do it. You'll wonder how you ever put up with tripods.
