Here's a blast from the past, dragged up from the internet archives: the very first space image I posted to the internet, back in 2012.

Details are a little sketchy - it was 14 years ago - but I think the setup was:

- **Camera:** Canon Digital Rebel (filter-modified for Ha sensitivity)
- **Scope:** SkyWatcher ED80
- **Mount:** Vixen GP with a GoTo upgrade kit

No plate solving. No autofocus. No autoguiding. Just me, in the garden, freezing my nuts off, tweaking things carefully throughout the night by hand.

## What That Actually Meant

In practical terms: point the telescope approximately at the target using the GoTo system (which would drift off due to polar alignment errors), check the frame, nudge the mount manually to recentre, take some exposures, check again, nudge again. For hours.

If a star was slightly trailed in a frame - because the mount drifted a fraction during the exposure, which it did, often - that frame got binned and the whole manual-recentre dance started again.

Stars had to be round. Getting round stars for more than about 90 seconds of exposure without autoguiding, on a budget mount with amateur polar alignment, in a back garden in Suffolk, required patience I no longer possess.

I'm not going back to that. Not even slightly. I'd much rather sit in my comfortable office with a glass of red wine, watching the guiding graphs tick over on a remote connection while the sequence runs itself.

## The Image

I've deliberately not re-shared the original here - it's very much a "first attempt" image and I've long since surpassed it - but the fact that I have it at all, rescued from Wayback Machine and 14-year-old EXIF data, is satisfying. Every astrophotographer has a first image. That was mine.

The interesting part isn't the image quality. It's that I was clearly already bitten. The equipment, the patience, the midnight garden sessions - that's not casual interest. That's how a 14-year hobby begins.

It explains a lot about how I ended up here.
