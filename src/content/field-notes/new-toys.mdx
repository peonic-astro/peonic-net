Six months of astrophotography had been convincing. Convincing enough to justify a new telescope.

## The Upgrade Itch

The SkyWatcher ED80 had served me well — a capable, affordable doublet apochromat that produced some genuinely solid results for the cost. But it's a starter scope, and I'd been staring at YouTube reviews of fancier refractors for approximately the entire time I'd owned it.

The William Optics Fluorstar 91 (FLT91) is a proper step up. A premium triplet apochromat — three elements of glass instead of two — which means significantly better colour correction, sharper stars corner-to-corner, and less chromatic aberration on bright targets. It's also, genuinely, quite pretty:

The build quality is in a different league. Every focuser rotation feels precision-machined. The finish is flawless. The weight is manageable. It feels like equipment rather than a tool.

## First Light: The Triangulum Galaxy

For a new scope's first light, I chose M33 — the Triangulum Galaxy. A broadband target; no narrowband filters needed, just a clean look at what the optics can do.

M33 is the Triangulum Galaxy — the third-largest member of the Local Group, sitting about 2.7 million light years away. It's a face-on spiral with prominent HII regions (the pink star-forming blobs in the arms) that show up nicely even in broadband imaging. Under very dark skies it's actually visible to the naked eye, making it one of the most distant objects you can see without a telescope.

4.5 hours of total exposure. Stars are sharper than I'd been getting from the ED80, and the colour fringing on bright stars is noticeably reduced. The money was well spent.

## What's Next

With the FLT91 in hand and the narrowband filters I'd already acquired, the setup is starting to get interesting. The next step is learning to really drive this combination — proper autoguiding, plate solving, automated focusing. The hardware is there. The workflow needs refinement.
