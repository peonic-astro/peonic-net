I've often joked that astrophotography is tech support in the dark. This session was the joke made flesh.

## The Plan

A long August night. The Veil Nebula perfectly placed. Four hours of narrowband data on the plan.

## What Actually Happened

What followed was a greatest hits album of minor irritations. Focus drift from temperature changes - an exposure set up looking perfect gradually degrading into soft-star blur as the night cooled. Guiding locks lost and re-established. NINA's sequence throwing minor tantrums about things that were fine five minutes ago. And at some point during all this, I fell asleep on the sofa instead of monitoring things, which did not help.

Usable frames from the session: 65 minutes. Out of a planned 240.

It's genuinely funny, in retrospect. In the moment it was less so.

## The Fix

I've now invested in an electronic automatic focuser (EAF). It's a motorised attachment that goes on the focuser drawtube and responds to software commands to adjust focus during a session. The critical capability: scheduled refocusing runs throughout the night, automatically compensating for the temperature-driven focus drift that killed half my frames here.

NINA can be configured to refocus on a schedule, or on a trigger (like when seeing metrics drop past a threshold). Set it up once, and the software runs the focuser for you. One less thing to babysit. One less reason to wake up to a folder full of soft-star garbage.

## The Veil, Briefly

The Veil Nebula in Cygnus is a supernova remnant - the expanding debris cloud from a stellar explosion that happened somewhere between 5,000 and 8,000 years ago, about 2,100 light years away. The "veil" is the shockwave still pushing through the interstellar medium, glowing where it compresses and heats the surrounding gas. In narrowband it's spectacular; even in 65 minutes of data you can see the filamentary structure in the Ha channel.

It's absolutely on the list for a proper revisit with the automated focuser and a night where I stay awake.
