First of three images from a memorable night. Also the night I finally made my peace with the Optolong L-Extreme.

## The Moon Problem (And the Solution)

Narrowband imaging is one of astrophotography's great gifts to backyard imagers: you can shoot through light pollution, through a bit of cloud haze, and - crucially - through moonlight. The L-Extreme is a dual narrowband filter for one-shot colour (OSC) cameras, transmitting only the Ha and OIII wavelengths where most emission nebulae glow. Everything else - sodium streetlights, the blue scattered light of the moon - gets cut.

This session ran during a 95% full moon. Under broadband conditions that would have been a write-off. With the L-Extreme, it was a productive night.

The filter works by mapping Ha to the red channel and OIII to the blue-green, mimicking what a mono camera with separate narrowband filters would produce. The result is what you'd call an HOO palette image: Ha in red, OIII in blue-green.

## Why the L-Extreme, Not the L-Enhance?

I've owned both Optolong dual narrowband filters for a while. The L-Enhance is the gentler version - wider passband, less aggressive filtration. The L-Extreme is narrower and more selective, which is more effective at killing moonlight and light pollution but has a reputation for producing nasty star halos with some optical combinations.

With my old SkyWatcher ED80, the L-Extreme was indeed halo-prone. The William Optics FLT91 handles it beautifully - no halos, clean stars. Side-by-side comparison confirmed it. The L-Extreme lives in front of the FLT91 from now on.

## The Target Itself

IC 410 sits in Auriga about 12,000 light years away, an active star-forming region centred on the open cluster NGC 1893. The two cometary globules that give it its name - the "tadpoles" - are dense pillars of gas being sculpted by radiation and stellar winds from the young cluster. They're actually being evaporated from the outside in, just very slowly.

I came back to this target in 2025 with a lot more data and better equipment. [That version](/astrophotography/tadpoles-mk2) is rather better. But this first attempt is where it started.
