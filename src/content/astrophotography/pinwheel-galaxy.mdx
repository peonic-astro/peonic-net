January is annual bonus month. January is also, it turns out, when astrophotography equipment vendors get their invoices paid.

## Big Chap Arrives

I'd been after something with more focal length for a while. The William Optics FLT91 is excellent — versatile, sharp, reasonably portable — but at 618mm it's not built for the smaller deep sky objects. I wanted reach.

Enter the Askar 130PHQ. A 5-inch apochromatic refractor delivering 1,000mm of focal length. It's a big lump — genuinely hefty to manhandle onto the mount solo — but the EQ6-R Pro handles it without complaint. I've stripped my beloved SkyWatcher ED80 for parts to kit out the new scope with all the automation gizmos I rely on: electronic focuser, filter wheel, the works.

The optical quality is superb. Every review I'd read said it was sharp corner-to-corner, and they weren't wrong.

## The Actual Image

M101 — the Pinwheel Galaxy — was technically the third target of the night, grabbed in something of a hurry when I realised my intended main target was about to disappear behind next door's fence. Galaxy season is approaching and I needed practice with the new scope on a proper galaxy target.

M101 is a grand design face-on spiral galaxy about 21 million light years away in Ursa Major. What makes it interesting for astrophotographers is its very extended spiral arms, visible in longer exposures, and the multiple HII regions (star-forming nebulae) that dot those arms in red. It's also had five documented supernovae since 1909, most recently in 2023 — the closest visible supernova in decades.

This is around 3.4 hours of LRGB data — respectable for a rushed third-target-of-the-night panic grab. Galaxy season proper starts soon, and I need to get much better at this. More hours, better processing, and maybe a planned approach rather than "quick, it's rising, shoot it before thinking."
