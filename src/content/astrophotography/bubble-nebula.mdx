Half the battle of astrophotography isn't taking the photo. It's everything that comes after.

## The Data

60 five-minute sub-exposures. Stacked, calibrated with flats and darks, and then: the fun begins. From the camera, individual subs are almost invisible — faint whispers of gradient that look like nothing worth keeping. Stacked and stretched, the structure starts to appear. But *how* you stretch and colour the data changes the image completely.

This year, the big revelation has been PixInsight. It has a steep learning curve — steep like a cliff — but once you get past the basics, it's genuinely an exceptional tool. I've been learning new techniques constantly, and the difference between my early results and what I'm getting now is stark.

The Bubble Nebula sits in Cassiopeia, about 7,100 light years away. It's literally a bubble: a spherical shell of ionised gas inflated by the stellar wind from a massive O-type star (BD+60 2522) inside it. That star is about 40 times the mass of our sun and blows a stellar wind so powerful it's been excavating this cavity for about 4 million years.

## The Two Versions

Same stack. Two different processing approaches.

The first version used traditional broadband processing: calibration, stacking, stretching, colour balance. Clean, natural-looking, honest about what the camera saw.

The second used Luke Newbould and Bill Blanshan's HOO false colour method — a clever technique that extracts the hydrogen emission signal from the red channel of a colour camera (even without a dedicated Ha filter) and maps it to create an HOO-like colour rendering. The broadband data already contains Ha signal, just mixed in with everything else. The technique separates it out and gives the image a more dramatic nebular colour rendition.

Both are valid interpretations of the same data. The HOO method is more visually striking. The traditional version is more faithful. I find it useful to do both and see which tells the better story.
