Being a digital packrat has its benefits. I never throw away subframes.

## The Original

The [Heart Nebula mosaic from September](/astrophotography/heart-nebula-mosaic) was my first image I was truly proud of - four panels stitched together, 5+ hours of L-Extreme data, the result of my first mosaic attempt. But it was produced with what I'd now call naive PixInsight skills. Good for the time, but I knew there was more in the data.

## New Tools, New Tricks

December brought a new toy to my processing toolkit: Russ Croman's BlurXTerminator. It's an AI-powered deconvolution and sharpening plugin that does genuinely magical things to subframe data - pulling detail out of blurry stars, sharpening nebula structures without introducing the ringing artifacts you get from classical deconvolution. Used carefully, it's transformative.

BlurXTerminator works alongside Croman's other AI plugins - StarXTerminator (for clean star removal during processing) and NoiseXTerminator (for noise reduction). All three are now permanent fixtures in my PixInsight workflow.

## IC 1805

The Heart Nebula is a vast emission nebula in Cassiopeia about 7,500 light years away. The heart shape - most visible in long-exposure images - is formed by stellar winds from the central cluster Melotte 15, which has been blowing a cavity in the surrounding molecular cloud for about 1.5 million years. The dense pillars and structures visible near the core are potential star-forming regions, sculpted by that same radiation pressure.

It's one of those targets where more hours always reveal more - you can go back indefinitely and keep finding new structure in the data. 15+ hours through the L-Extreme is a solid base. I'll come back to this one with the mono camera and proper narrowband filters eventually.
