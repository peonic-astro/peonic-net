This post has a history. The original June 2022 image was my first serious attempt at this target — shot with a cooled colour camera and a dual narrowband filter, shortly after upgrading from a frankly awful non-cooled setup. I came back to it properly in 2023 with better equipment and more data, and this is that version.

## The Target

IC 1396 is a massive emission nebula in Cepheus, about 2,400 light years away, powered by a hot O-type star (HD 206267) at its centre. The whole complex spans several degrees on the sky. The star's ultraviolet radiation is ionising the surrounding hydrogen and simultaneously driving stellar winds that are reshaping the molecular cloud.

The "trunk" itself (vdB 142) is a dark cometary globule — a finger of dense dust and gas that's resisting the onslaught of radiation. The reason it still exists is that the dust at the tip of the trunk is shielding the material behind it, like holding up an umbrella in a hurricane. The tip is being slowly eroded, and active star formation is thought to be occurring within it, triggered by the very radiation that's destroying it from outside.

## The Journey

The June 2022 version was shot with the ZWO 183MC (a non-cooled colour camera). The noise levels and dark frame nightmare that camera imposed were the final push I needed to switch to a proper cooled ASI 533MC Pro. That original image was decent, but the equipment limitations were obvious in the result.

This version — about 11 hours of narrowband data through the mono 533MM Pro — is what I always wanted from this target. The SHO palette brings out the ionisation structure nicely: the Ha-rich shell glowing golden-orange, the OIII tracing the thinner ionised oxygen in the surrounding regions, the SII highlighting the denser shocked zones near the trunk's boundary.

A target I keep coming back to. Almost certainly will again.
