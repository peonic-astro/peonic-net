Sometimes this hobby flows beautifully. Sometimes it feels like IT support in a field, at night, with frostbitten fingers.

## Night One: Mosaic Attempt

The Rosette Nebula is one of my favourites — a vast ring of glowing hydrogen in Monoceros, about 5,000 light years from Earth, surrounding a young cluster of hot stars. At the focal length of the FLT91 you need multiple panels to capture it all. I set up for a mosaic run and... horrible star shapes. Egg-shaped, streaked, wrong. Classic tracking failure signatures, but the guiding software was showing everything as nominal.

I put it down to a loose clutch on the mount, tightened things up, and went to bed.

## Night Two: More Eggs

The first three hours of the second night were perfect. Five-minute subs, round stars, guiding holding steady below 0.7" RMS. Then — eggs again. Same problem, same baffling "everything looks fine" from the guiding data.

Switched targets, reduced sub length to three minutes, and the rest of the night went without incident. Clouded out at 5am with something salvageable.

The working theory: power. January had been cold — down to -5°C overnight — which means the dew heaters were running hard to keep the optics from icing up. My theory is the extra current draw was leaving the mount slightly underpowered, causing it to track inaccurately even though it was reporting fine. Dropping the heater aggressiveness and reducing sub length meant the power budget balanced out.

I wasn't completely sure though. The next night I ran the mount from a separate power supply and went back to 5-minute subs to confirm. The [Pacman Nebula that followed](/astrophotography/pacman-nebula) was the test.

## The Rosette Itself

While I was busy fighting my mount, it's worth appreciating what I was pointing at. NGC 2237 is one of the largest emission nebulae visible from Earth — roughly 130 light years across and about 5,200 light years away. The ring structure forms because radiation and stellar winds from the central cluster NGC 2244 have blown a cavity in the surrounding molecular cloud. The edges of that cavity are where the hydrogen glows brightest. A target I'll keep returning to.
