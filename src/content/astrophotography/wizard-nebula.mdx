September was a write-off. Clear nights appeared on the forecast and then mysteriously didn't materialise, or cleared only long enough to taunt me before clouding over again. On the nights it *was* clear, we had prior commitments. The astronomy season was ticking away.

Desperate to shoot *something*, I grabbed the first clear gap in October and got two hours on the Wizard Nebula before the window closed.

## The Target

NGC 7380 sits in Cepheus, about 7,200 light years away. It's an open star cluster embedded in an emission nebula - the cluster's young hot stars are the power source illuminating and sculpting the surrounding gas. The "wizard" silhouette is a pareidolia effect (like most of these names) that's most visible in processed narrowband images where the contrast between the emission regions and dark dust lanes is at its highest.

This being broadband data with the colour camera, the wizard's profile is rather more subtle than it would be in narrowband. It's roughly 2 hours of integration, which is nowhere near enough to do the target justice. But it exists, which is something.

## Notes

The late start to astronomical darkness in September/October from East Anglia means the summer imaging window runs from maybe midnight to 3am by the time the sky gets genuinely dark. Then you're racing the brightening dawn. Every clear night matters, even the short ones.

This is on the list for a proper narrowband revisit when the seasons are more cooperative.
